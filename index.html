THE INTRINCACIES OF GOOD COFFEE

Where and How Coffee is Made, and What Makes it Good

More than 400 billion cups of coffee are consumed by people each year, making it one of the most popular beverages in the world. Despite its appearance looking pretty much the same in liquid form, coffee is surprisingly very diverse in its taste where there is a little something for everyone.

Quality of Coffee

First, we will introduce the countries affiliated with the Coffee Quality Institute and have a look at the average scores for the quality of coffee in each of the relevant countries using this interactive map on the right here where you can click & hold to drag and scroll to zoom to have a closer look at the countries.


From the interactive map, we can clearly see that Brazil has the worst quality of coffee while Madagascar has the best quality of coffee among all the relevant countries.

Harvest and Lifetime of Coffee

Here, we examine whether altitude has an effect on the quality of coffee as well as the number of bags produced and the lifetime of coffee in relevant countries.

The filter dropdown at the bottom can be used to change between the attributes The interactive map also allows zooming in and out of the map, as well as centering on different parts of the map.


From the map, we can see that Altitude most likely does not have an effect on coffee quality. Higher quality of coffee is produced in smaller numbers and most coffee last around a year.

The Different Varieties, Colors and Processing Methods of Coffee

There are a large amount of varieties of coffee around the world, each with its own unique brand of taste. In this section, we introduce the top 12 most frequent coffee varieties Throughout the rest of the visualisation, we will be focusing on these 12 coffee varieties.


From the chart, we can see that Gesha and Caturra are the most frequent varieties of coffee while Java, SL14, Yellow Bourbon and SHG are the least frequent varieties of coffee.


Among all the colors, every coffee variety has a Green variation while Brownish-Green and Pale Yellow variations only appear in one single variety of coffee each, Catimor for Brownish-Green and Gesha for Pale Yellow.


As for their processing methods, the most widely used processing method for coffee is Washed / Wet, followed by Natural / Dry and Pulped Natural / Honey, then the rest.

Comparison of Coffee Varieties

Here, we will be introducing the overall average statistical attributes for each of the 12 coffee varieties and seeing how a variety differs from another.


From the chart, we can observe that each of the attributes for most of the 12 coffee varieties average to a similar threshold which is around 7.5 points.


An interesting observation here is that all of the 12 coffee varieties have an average score of 10 for the attributes Clean Cup, Sweetness and Uniformity which is the maximum.

Finding Trends in Statistical Coffee Attributes

In this final section, we will be attempting to find trends through the correlation of different statistical attributes for coffee using different modes of visualisation.


From the bar chart, we can observe that Gesha has the highest average score among all the varieties of coffee while Mundo Novo has the lowest average score.


In the histogram heatmap, filters are provided to find possible trends between attributes. From the histogram heatmap, we can observe that Moisture Percentage does not have an effect on the quality of coffee.


Finally, in the interactive trellis scatter plot, we want to observe whether there are any interesting trends or details in the correlation between the various statistical attributes and the average scores for each variety of coffee. can be filtered through the legend for specific varieties of coffee. The plot also allows for synchronous panning (via Dragging), zooming (via Scrolling) and brushing (via pressing Shift + Dragging)


An interesting detail found is that among all the 12 varieties of coffee, only one variation of Gesha has a Uniformity score of below 10, making it an outlier.
